Cell Membranes and Diffusion

One way cell membranes achieve this is passively through a simple process called diffusion

Diffusion is the movement of particles across a gradient, if we are talking about a concentration gradient than particles travel from higher concentrations to lower concentrations Diffusion is simple because it is the natural tendency for “stuff” to spread out. When we think of diffusion we tend to think of isolated local system, within this system diffusion can be more formally defined as the net movement of particles from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration. When a particle moves from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration it is said to have moved down its concentration gradient When we talk about the diffusion of particles we specifically are interested in matter in a liquid or gas state as solids are chemical bound and unable to freely move. We call diffusion a passive process because there isn’t any energy input required. As the universe is always moving towards a more chaotic state the particles naturally spread out Some books will say that cell membranes hold the cell together, it is true that they “contain” the cell by creating an isolated environment where cellular processes can occur however holding the cell together is the role of cytoskeletal components. Diffusion is a selectively permeable process as it only has gaps in the membrane large enough for only very small molecules to pass through like the action of a sieve. Some small molecules that can cross include glucose, amino acids, water, oxygen and carbon dioxide. Larger molecules like starch and proteins cannot fit through. Starch and proteins are examples of big molecules that cannot pass through.

    There are many different kinds of cells including bacteria, protozoa, algae, animal, and plant cells to name only a few. Unicellular organisms such as bacteria, protozoa and algae as the name implies are single cell organisms whereas multicellular organisms such as ourselves are made up of a vast array of specialized cells. The differences in cells can be easily identified from comparisons of sub-cellular structures termed organelles.

    How to use a microscope

    • 1. Clip the slide onto the stage
      2. look into the eye piece, it can be hard at first to find the light distance and position needed to be able to look into a microscope comfortably with both eyes.
      3. Use the coarse adjustment knob to move the stage up or down bringing the specimen into and out of focus.
      4. At this point the specimen can be soft focused, we use the fine adjustment knob to make small changes in stage height to bring the specimen into sharp focus.
      5. When you swap the magnification you need to repeat the above steps.
